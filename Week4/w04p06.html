Theory Reflection (Week 4 Part 6)

  • A) What is the difference between Phong shading and Phong lighting (the Phong reflection model)?

    • Phong lighting is a reflection model used to calculate the light intensity on a surface by considering ambient, diffuse, and specular components.
    The latter makes objects appear shiny by simulating the reflection of light sources.

    Phong shading, on the other hand, refers to interpolating surface normals and calculating the light intensity at each pixel to achieve smooth shading.
    This is in contrast to the Gourad shading prior to it, which linearly interpolates the triangle meshes of the object to determine pixel color.

    Phong shading is computationally more expensive but produces more realistic results, as is evident from parts 4 to 5 of this worksheet.

  • B) What is the difference between flat shading, Gouraud shading, and Phong shading?

    • Flat shading: Simplest form of shading as the lighting is calculated once per polygon. It's fast, but the results are unrealistic and faceted.
    Gouraud shading: Linearly interpolates across triangle vertices. As previously stated, it is more smooth than Simple Shading and faster than Phong, but the specular highlights can "miss" if they fall between vertices.
    Phong shading: Normals are interpolated across the surface and lighting computed on a pixel-wise level. As stated, it's smoother than its counterparts, but is more expensive computationally.

    Phong shading is the best suited for highlight simulation due to its per-pixel computation of the lighting using \(s\).

  • C) What is the difference between a directional light and a point light?

    • Directional light is akin to sunlight as it originates from a source with an infinite distance (or very long distance away) with parallel rays, while point light emanates from a specific point in all directions, such as close-by illumination objects like a light-bulb.

  • D) Does the eye position influence the shading of an object in any way?

    • For sure, it affects the specular reflection! The dot product between the observer's direction \(w_o\) and the reflection vector \(w_r\) determines the intensity of the specular highlight, e.g. large values create a more shiny surface intensity.

  • E) What is the effect of setting the specular term to (0, 0, 0)?

    • This removes all specular reflections, making the surface appear completely matte, which can be seen with use of the sliders on the worksheet.

  • F) What is the effect of increasing the shininess exponent \(\alpha\)?

    • This would result in a smaller and more focused highlight zone, making the object appear more glossy, while lower values make the highlight surface larger.

  • G) In what coordinate space did you compute the lighting?

    • The lighting was computed in view space, as both the position and normals were transformed using the view and model matrices.
